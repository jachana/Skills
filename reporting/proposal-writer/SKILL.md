# Write a proposal

A proposal is a persuasion document with a number at the end. It must be credible, specific, and easy to say yes to.

## Language / Idioma
Write in the client's language. Ask if unclear (the user may speak Spanish but pitch an English-speaking client).

## Gather inputs first (ask only what you can't infer)
- **Client + their problem** — who, and the pain in their words.
- **Scope** — what's explicitly in, and what's explicitly out (out-of-scope prevents disputes).
- **Deliverables** — concrete artifacts the client receives.
- **Timeline** — phases/milestones with rough dates.
- **Commercials** — price model (fixed / time & materials / retainer), amount, payment terms, currency.
- **Proof** — relevant past work, capabilities. Pull from the second-brain (vault `Projects/`, `.brain/` context) rather than inventing credentials. **Never fabricate references, logos, or case studies.**

If a critical field (price model, deadline, decision-maker) is missing, ask before drafting.

## Structure
1. **Cover / title** — client name, project title, date, your name/org.
2. **Executive summary** — 3–4 sentences: their problem → your solution → the outcome they get. Written so a busy decision-maker can approve from this alone.
3. **Understanding / context** — restate their problem in their words. Proves you listened. (This is what wins trust.)
4. **Proposed solution** — what you'll do, at the right altitude for the reader. Tie each piece back to a stated need.
5. **Scope & deliverables** — bulleted, concrete. Include an explicit **Out of scope** list.
6. **Timeline** — phases/milestones table with dates or durations.
7. **Investment / Inversión** — the price, the model, what's included, payment schedule, validity ("valid for 30 days").
8. **Assumptions & dependencies** — what you need from them (access, data, sign-offs) and what you're assuming.
9. **Why us** — short, evidence-backed (real prior work from the brain). No fluff.
10. **Next step / Call to action** — exactly what happens when they say yes (sign here, kickoff date). Make it one easy action.

## Style
- Client-centric: "you/your" more than "we/our". Their outcome leads; your activity supports it.
- Specific and quantified: dates, counts, deltas, money — vague proposals read as risky.
- Confident, not hedged. State what you'll deliver.
- Honest about scope boundaries and assumptions — this protects both sides and signals professionalism.
- Format: clean Markdown; offer to render a branded PDF/HTML (pair with **frontend-design**).

## Guardrails
- ⚠ Do not invent capabilities, certifications, team members, or case studies. If the brain has no evidence for a claim, leave it out or flag it for the user to confirm.
- ⚠ Do not commit to a price or deadline the user hasn't approved — present it as a draft for their sign-off.
- ✅ End by listing every assumption you made so the user can correct it before it goes out.
